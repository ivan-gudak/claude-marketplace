# Dynatrace accessibility and inclusive language

Rules for writing content that respects all users.
Sources: styleguide.dynatrace.com/docs/accessibility-and-inclusivity/inclusive-language,
/docs/accessibility-and-inclusivity/internationalization

---

## Core principle

Inclusive language respects all people, acknowledges diversity, is sensitive to
differences, and promotes equal opportunities. Before writing about a human
characteristic (gender, ability, orientation, religion, age, race), ask whether
it's truly relevant.

---

## Ableist language — avoid

These words characterise people by ability or suggest disability is abnormal:

| ❌ Avoid | ✅ Alternative |
|---|---|
| disable / enable | turn off / turn on, deactivate / activate |
| crazy, insane | unexpected, confusing, surprising |
| sanity (check) | confidence check, validation |
| blind to, turn a blind eye | unaware of, overlook |
| cripple | impair, break, degrade |
| dummy | placeholder, sample, test |

---

## Racist language — avoid

Terms with historical roots in oppression:

| ❌ Forbidden | ✅ Alternative |
|---|---|
| blacklist | blocklist, denylist, exclude list |
| whitelist | allowlist, approved list, safe list |
| master (tech context) | primary, main, source |
| slave | replica, secondary, worker |
| native (referring to people) | Use specific group names |

---

## Gendered language — avoid

Use gender-neutral pronouns unless referring to a specific individual:

- ✅ their, them, they
- ❌ he, she, his, her, his or her

Avoid male-oriented terminology:

| ❌ Avoid | ✅ Alternative |
|---|---|
| chairman | chair, chairperson, moderator |
| spokesman | spokesperson |
| manpower | workforce, personnel, staff |
| mankind | people, humanity |

---

## Diversity in examples

- Represent diverse names, places, holidays, sports, ages, family units, and
  occupations in examples.
- Avoid Westernised defaults like "Jane Doe" / "John Doe."
- Avoid local slang, idioms, and insider jargon.

---

## Internationalization (i18n)

### Writing for global audiences

- **Use familiar words.** Avoid academic or specialised words.
- **Be concise.** Remove unnecessary words.
- **Keep sentences short** and end them with periods.
- **Don't use all available space.** Translations may need more room.
- **Write in active voice** so subject-verb-object is clear.
- **Don't use gendered pronouns** unless referring to a specific person.
- **Avoid jokes and idioms** — they often don't translate across cultures.

### Localisation considerations

- Numbers and currencies in local style?
- Dates and times in local format?
- Words and spellings appropriate for the audience?
- Layout correct for reading direction (LTR/RTL)?
- Images depicting local/relatable scenes and people?
- References relevant and accessible to the target audience?

### Do not translate

- App names (written in title case)
- Trademarked terms (capitalised, with ® or ™)
- Universal industry terms (verify with Microsoft Terminology Search + 2 other sources)
